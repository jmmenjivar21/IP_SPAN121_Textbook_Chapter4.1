Gramática · Two key topics

ser vs. estar vs. haber · question words · (G2 comparatives previews Week 9)

G1SER vs. ESTAR vs. HABER (hay)

These three verbs all relate to "being" or "existing" — but they are NOT interchangeable.

SER

= identity · permanent traits
Origin: El campus es de 1890.
Identity: Es una universidad pública.
Permanent: Es grande.
Profession: Soy estudiante.

ESTAR

= location · state · condition
Location: La biblioteca está en el centro.
State: El baño está limpio.
Emotion: Estoy nervioso hoy.
Progressive: Estamos estudiando.

HABER → hay

= existence · there is / there are
Existence: Hay una cafetería nueva.
There are: Hay diez edificios.
Question: ¿Hay una sala de estudio cerca?
Note: hay never changes form!
KEY · Location of People/Things vs. Events

Use estar for the location of people and things. Use ser for the location of events: La fiesta es en mi apartamento.

¡OJO!Memory trick — ESCAPA for ESTAR

If a sentence fits any of these six categories → use estar. Everything else → ser.

G3Question Words · Palabras interrogativas

All interrogatives carry a written accent. Pair the word with the right use.

Word · MeaningUseExample
¿qué? — what?objects, definitions¿Qué hay en el barrio?
¿quién/es? — who?people¿Quién es tu compañero/a de clase?
¿dónde? — where?location¿Dónde está la biblioteca?
¿cuándo? — when?time¿Cuándo cierra la cafetería?
¿cómo? — how? what is … like?manner / description¿Cómo es tu habitación?
¿cuánto/a? — how much?quantity (uncountable)¿Cuánto cuesta el autobús?
¿cuántos/as? — how many?quantity (countable)¿Cuántos estudiantes viven aquí?
¿por qué? — why?reason¿Por qué prefieres ese café?
¿cuál/es? — which? which one?choice among options¿Cuál es tu restaurante favorito?
¿Qué? vs. ¿Cuál?

Both can translate as "what" — but they're different. Use ¿qué? to ask for a definition (¿Qué es una piscina?). Use ¿cuál? to pick from a set of options (¿Cuál es tu lugar favorito?). With + ser, Spanish almost always uses cuál, not qué.

Nota Cultural · Lugares hispanos

Plazas, campus y vida pública en el mundo hispanohablante

La plaza central · Heart of the city

La plaza: el corazón de cada pueblo

Almost every town and city in the Spanish-speaking world is organized around a plaza central — a public square that serves as the geographic, social, and historical heart of the community. The model dates back to colonial Spanish city planning: a central square flanked by the church, the town hall, and the main businesses.

The plaza is where people gather to eat, talk, protest, celebrate, and simply pasear (stroll). In Mexico City it's the Zócalo; in Madrid, the Plaza Mayor; in Buenos Aires, the Plaza de Mayo. Knowing the plaza means knowing the city.

Zócalo (CDMX) Plaza Mayor (Madrid) Plaza de Mayo (BA) Plaza de Armas (Lima) Plaza San Francisco (Quito)
¿Sabías qué…? DATOS
1In most Spanish-speaking countries, "campus" is a borrowed word — the historic term is ciudad universitaria. Mexico's UNAM and Madrid's Complutense both call their main areas Ciudad Universitaria.
2Spanish universities don't typically have a single fenced campus. Faculties (facultades) are spread across the city, integrated with the urban fabric — students move between buildings on the metro.
3UNAM's Ciudad Universitaria in Mexico City is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — celebrated for its 1950s modernist architecture and Diego Rivera murals.
4In many Latin American cities, the cafetería isn't a campus food hall — it's a corner café serving espresso and pastries. The student dining hall is more often called el comedor.
